The problem

You cannot trade size in the open.

  • Post a $4M block on a public venue and the order — plus every competing bid — leaks.
  • Front-running, market impact, alpha gone the moment it touches a mempool.
  • Today's workaround: OTC desks and chat rooms. Trust a middleman, keep no clean audit trail.
  • $30B+ a month still trades this way.
The bind

Fair price discovery requires sealed bids.

A transparent ledger makes sealed bids impossible — unless you bolt on a cryptography stack.

What Bisik is

A sealed-bid RFQ desk where the guarantees are code, not a middleman.

  • A buyer requests quotes from a chosen dealer panel. The market never sees the RFQ.
  • Each dealer answers with a sealed quote — rival dealers never receive it.
  • The dealer's asset locks into escrow: it cannot be double-sold mid-auction.
  • The buyer awards: cheapest ask wins, paid the second-cheapest price (reverse Vickrey).
  • Settlement is atomic DvP — cash and asset move together or not at all.
  • A regulator observes executed trades — and only executed trades.
Why Vickrey

Winner is paid the runner-up's price, so dealers can quote their true reserve without shading.

On a transparent chain this needs ZK or FHE. Here it is ~40 lines of Daml.

Three settlement rails

Competitive Vickrey · direct bilateral OTC · partial fills on both. Plus multi-instrument baskets, atomically.

The institutional payoff

Provable best execution — with no public order book.

  • Counterparties selectively disclose their sealed asks to the regulator — buyer and dealer can each reveal.
  • The regulator recomputes: did the executed price beat every competing ask?
  • Confidential pre-trade. Provable post-trade. Both, at once.
  • This is the compliance obligation (MiFID-style best execution) that normally forces a transparent venue.

Two-step transfer: propose → accept / reject / withdraw, with an expiry. Funds are never stranded.

Allocation → DvP

Reserve a leg, then settle two legs in one transaction. Neither side can claim unilaterally.

Deployed as its own package, verified on-ledger: a two-step transfer instruction and an atomic delivery-versus-payment swap, both executed live on Canton Devnet.

Caught by adversarial QA
  • Escrow was unilaterally revocable — a dealer could pull collateral while its quote stayed live.
  • No issuer binding — a dealer could quote a self-issued counterfeit bond.
  • DvP was not atomic: a receiver could take a leg without delivering theirs.
  • Each one is now a named regression test.
bisik.
Honest scope

What is guaranteed, and what is not.

Enforced on-ledger
  • Rivals never receive each other's quotes.
  • A dealer is never paid below its own ask.
  • Both legs move, or neither does.
  • Escrow cannot be pulled back unilaterally.
  • A quote settles the RFQ it was made against — no other.
  • Issuers are checked; counterfeits are rejected.
Left to the auctioneer

Only the buyer sees every sealed quote, so only the buyer can run the auction. It can curate the awarded set and clear at the winner's own ask — suppressing the Vickrey uplift, though never underpaying a dealer.

Forcing the true second price needs a trusted auctioneer or MPC — and MPC re-introduces exactly the cryptography Canton lets us skip. We would rather state the limit than overclaim.
